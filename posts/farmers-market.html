What Keeps Shoppers Coming Back to Waverly

A white notecard, no bigger than a hand, reads “$3.00” in thick black marker. It sits propped against a pile of produce, slightly bent from the morning breeze. All the other fruits around it have the same signage pattern, although different prices. No fancy handwriting, no elaborate drawings, not even an item description. Around it, shoppers drift past, pausing for only mere moments before moving on.

In the Waverly Farmer’s Market in Baltimore, vendors have only seconds to catch someone’s attention, and they do it without traditional advertising.

At first glance, it may seem like the vibrant colors of fruit and the uneven shapes of freshly picked vegetables are what draw people in. But while these visuals catch the eye, they are not what keep customers coming back. Trust, familiarity, and relationships built over time matter more.

For vendors, attracting customers depends less on promotion and more on connection. Anna Kent, who has sold plant-based meals and meal preparation for five years through her business Tom Cat’s Kitchen, describes her approach simply: “Largely word of mouth.” While she posts menus online and has a website, she states that most customers come through personal interaction rather than targeted ads.

“Most are regular,” Anna says, noting that repeat customers are the core of her business, with a lot signing up for weekly subscriptions. At her booth, the focus is not on selling aggressively but on creating a welcoming environment. “Most of it is just customer service. Try not to be pushy,” she says. In a space where shoppers only glance for a moment, it is not advertising that sustains a business, but trust built through consistent, personal exchange.

From the shopper’s perspective, advertising also plays a small role. One regular visitor, Richard Davidson, describes their choices as driven almost entirely by the product itself. When asked what makes them stop, they point to appearance: “Initial looks… like if it looks good.” Rather than reading signs, shoppers respond to what is immediately visible: the color, texture, and freshness of the food.

Richard returns to a pastry stand because “it’s always fresh and has good prices.” Reflecting on what stands out, he says, “there’s this air of freshness about it.” Following up in a later text message he adds, “The main appeal is the experience. You talk to real people and see their work, not just some labels on shelves”. Instead of branding or promotions, the experience of quality builds trust over time.

While companies across the United States spends around 8 billion each year on advertising according to Stanford Business, the farmer’s market operates differently. Vendors rely on word of mouth and returning customers, while shoppers make decisions based on experience rather than branding. Instead of persuading people to buy, vendors create conditions that encourage them to return.

At first glance, the farmer’s market may seem under-advertised. However, it is a system built on something more than marketing strategies. What draws people in may be the color of the produce, but what keeps them coming back is trust. In a world saturated with constant advertising, the Waverly Farmer’s Market suggests that connection and consistency can be just as powerful.

As Anna puts it, “If people trust you, they come back. That’s really all it is.”
